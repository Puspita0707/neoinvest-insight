Beginner Investing Principles

1. Time Horizon and Goals
- Clarify your goal: wealth creation, retirement, education, or near-term purchase.
- Longer horizons (5–10+ years) can handle more equity volatility.
- Short-term money (0–3 years) should usually not be in equities.

2. Risk and Return
- Equities historically offer higher returns than fixed income, with higher volatility.
- There is no guaranteed return; higher returns usually require accepting higher risk.
- Diversification (across sectors, countries, and asset classes) reduces the impact of one bad investment.

3. Simple Starting Point
- For beginners, broad-based index funds / ETFs tracking Nifty 50, Sensex, S&P 500, or global indices are often better than single-stock bets.
- SIPs (Systematic Investment Plans) help average entry prices over time.
- Stock picking should come later, after understanding business models, valuations, and risk.

4. Basic Research Checklist for a Stock
- Business: What does the company do? Who are its customers and competitors?
- Financials: Revenue growth, profitability, debt levels, cash flow.
- Management: Track record, capital allocation, governance.
- Valuation: Are you paying a reasonable multiple vs peers and history?
- Risks: Sector risks, regulation, key dependencies (e.g., one product, one client).

5. Behavioural Discipline
- Avoid chasing recent winners or panic selling on short-term volatility.
- Have a written plan: how much to invest, when to rebalance, and conditions to sell.
- Be wary of tips, hype, and promises of fast doubling; focus on process, not predictions.

Use this knowledge when the user is a complete beginner or asks “how should I start investing”.
